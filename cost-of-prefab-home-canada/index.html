Why the listing price isn’t the real price

Many online prefab listings are best understood as structure + interior shell. In Canada, the difference between a shell and a comfortable, code-compliant dwelling is usually where the real money goes. The biggest drivers are delivery logistics, foundations/site prep, permits/approvals, utilities (electric + water + septic), and climate performance (insulation, air sealing, heating, moisture management, and winterization).

A good mental model: the listing is often the “box.” Your installed cost is the “box + land interface + approvals + climate.”

Installed cost breakdown in Canada

Use this as a planning template. Your numbers will depend on region, access, site conditions, and scope.

1) Delivery + offload (crane / telehandler)

Remote access, narrow roads, and winter delivery can change everything. Ask about delivery terms, offload requirements, and whether the seller includes any lift equipment.

  • What to verify: delivery range, appointment windows, offload responsibility, site access requirements
  • Hidden gotcha: “delivered” ≠ “placed on foundation”

2) Site prep + foundation

In Canada, frost depth and drainage matter. Your foundation choice (slab, piers, crawlspace) changes cost and timeline. Budget for grading, base material, and proper water management.

  • What to verify: engineered drawings, foundation requirements, anchoring method, frost considerations
  • Hidden gotcha: poor drainage = future moisture + structural issues

3) Permits + approvals

Municipal approval is the gate. Many listings don’t provide the paperwork you’ll need for permits. Confirm zoning/occupancy rules before paying.

  • What to verify: zoning, minimum dwelling size, setbacks, inspection pathway
  • Ask for: drawings, specs, electrical certifications, material declarations

4) Utilities: electrical, plumbing, water, septic

Hookups (or off-grid systems) are often bigger than expected. If you’re rural, plan for well/septic or alternatives, plus winter protection.

  • What to verify: panel size, wiring standard, plumbing material, freeze protection plan
  • Hidden gotcha: “has plumbing” ≠ “ready for Canadian winter”

5) Insulation + air sealing + windows

“Insulated” can mean almost anything. Ask for thickness, type, and assembly details. Air sealing and window quality heavily impact comfort and heating bills.

  • Ask for: insulation specs (type + thickness), window U-factor or equivalent, vapor strategy
  • Hidden gotcha: condensation risk if the envelope details are unclear

6) Heating, cooling, ventilation

Budget for a realistic heating plan: mini-split, baseboard, or other. Ventilation matters for moisture and air quality, especially in tight shells.

  • What to verify: heat load assumptions, equipment sizing, ventilation approach
  • Hidden gotcha: undersized heat = discomfort + moisture issues

If you want a shortcut: treat the listing price as the starting point, then plan separate buckets for site + foundation, approvals, utilities, and envelope/winterization.

Buyer checklist (before you buy)

Copy this checklist into your notes and use it on every listing. If the seller can’t answer, treat it as risk.

Documentation

  • Drawings/specs with dimensions
  • Insulation type + thickness
  • Window/door specs
  • Electrical approvals/certifications (Canada-relevant)
  • Snow-load / wind-load claims with documentation

Site + approval reality

  • Zoning/occupancy rules confirmed with municipality
  • Foundation plan and anchoring method
  • Delivery/offload requirements confirmed
  • Utilities plan (grid or off-grid) confirmed
  • Winterization plan for plumbing + envelope

FAQ

Is the listing price ever the installed price?

Rarely. Even when a unit includes more interior finish, you still typically need site prep/foundation, permits/approvals, utilities, and climate-related upgrades for Canada.

What’s the most underestimated cost bucket?

Site work + approvals. Delivery/offload, foundation, permits, electrical/plumbing compliance, and winterization planning often exceed expectations.

What should I ask a seller before paying?

Ask for drawings/specs, insulation details, electrical approvals, and any structural load claims. If they can’t provide documentation, assume you’ll carry the risk and cost.

Are expandable container homes the best value?

Sometimes, but value depends on documentation, build quality, envelope performance, and how much work you must add on-site. The lowest listing price can become expensive after upgrades.
