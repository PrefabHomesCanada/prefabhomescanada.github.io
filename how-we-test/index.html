How We Test (Canada, 2026)

Expandable and modular prefab homes sold online can look “move-in ready” in photos, but many are closer to a structure + interior shell. Our methodology is designed to help Canadian buyers focus on what actually matters: documentation, climate reality, and installed cost drivers.

Our testing principles

Online listings can omit key details. Our method treats missing specs as risk until proven with documentation.

1) Documentation beats marketing

We prioritize written specs: insulation type/thickness, structural details, windows/doors, electrical approvals, and any load claims.

2) Canadian climate is non-negotiable

We consider winter performance, air sealing, thermal bridging, and realistic heating strategies for cold regions.

3) Installed cost matters more than sticker price

Delivery, offload, foundation, permits, utilities, and winterization can dominate the budget. We highlight the big drivers.

4) “Not specified” is treated as unknown

If a listing doesn’t clearly state a spec, we don’t guess. Missing specs reduce score because they add risk.

What we verify (or attempt to verify)

These are the areas that most often decide whether a project is realistic in Canada. If a seller can’t provide written details, we assume you’ll spend more time and money closing the gap.

Building envelope

  • Insulation: type + thickness (walls/roof/floor)
  • Air sealing: stated methods or details
  • Windows/doors: specs, glazing, seals

Structure + loads

  • Frame: material + corrosion protection
  • Roof: design and claims about snow load
  • Drawings: any stamped/engineered docs (rare)

Utilities readiness

  • Electrical: CSA/UL/ETL approval claims (verify)
  • Plumbing: rough-ins vs finished fixtures
  • HVAC: realistic plan for your climate zone

Logistics

  • Shipping: what’s included, lead time, delivery method
  • Offload: forklift/crane requirements
  • Placement: access constraints and site prep needs

Practical rule: if a listing can’t provide clear documentation, treat it like buying a shell and budget accordingly.

What we don’t assume

Some claims are common on online prefab listings. Unless a seller provides documentation, we treat these as unproven.

“Winter ready”

We don’t assume winter readiness without insulation specs, air sealing details, and window/door performance.

“Legal everywhere”

We don’t assume zoning or occupancy approval. Municipal rules vary widely across Canada.

“Bathroom included”

We don’t assume plumbing hookups. Often it means fixtures or a room, not a compliant water/wastewater plan.

“Snow-load rated”

We don’t assume roof load suitability without region-specific documentation and structural details.

Scorecard: how we rank listings

We score each listing across practical categories. Higher scores usually mean less risk and fewer unknowns, not necessarily “the cheapest”.

Why Canada is different (quick reality)

Prefab content online is often US-centric or generic. In Canada, the big friction points are climate loads, insulation expectations, frost depth, and municipal approvals.

Frost + foundations

Freeze-thaw cycles make foundations a major cost driver. Many areas require engineered bases for dwellings.

Snow + roof loads

Roof structure claims must match your region. Don’t rely on generic “snow rated” wording.

Utilities + inspections

Even with approvals, trenching, panels, hookups, and inspections are local. Plan time and budget.

Envelope first

Insulation + air sealing is usually the best ROI. A leaky shell makes everything else harder.
