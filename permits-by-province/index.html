Permits by Province (Canada)

Published: Jan 1, 2026 · Last updated: Jan 1, 2026

“Do I need a permit?” is the wrong first question. The real question is: Can this unit be approved on my property, for my intended use? This guide helps you ask the right questions, collect the right documents, and avoid the classic trap: paying for a listing before you know if your municipality will approve it.

The fastest path to “Yes, this is approvable”

Permits aren’t a single checkbox. Think of them as a chain: zoning → drawings → foundation plan → inspections. If one link is unknown, your timeline and budget become unknown.

  1. Confirm zoning + use: seasonal vs year-round, main dwelling vs accessory dwelling, minimum size, setbacks.
  2. Confirm what docs they require: site plan, drawings, foundation plan, specs, sometimes engineering.
  3. Confirm utilities pathway: electrical inspection path, plumbing approach, septic/well approvals if applicable.
  4. Only then: pay a seller deposit or commit to a model.

Documents municipalities usually ask for

Requirements vary, but the pattern is consistent: municipalities want enough information to judge use, siting, structure, and safety. This list helps you pressure-test a seller’s “fully compliant” claims.

1) Site plan (where it goes)

  • Includes: lot lines, setbacks, driveway/access, placement location, sometimes grading/drainage notes.
  • Why it matters: setbacks and access rules are common deal-breakers.
  • Tip: ask if they require a survey or if a scaled sketch is acceptable for your case.

2) Drawings (what it is)

  • Common: floor plan, elevations, dimensions, door/window placements.
  • Why it matters: municipalities can’t approve “marketing photos.”
  • Seller test: if they can’t provide drawings for the exact model, assume extra work/cost.

3) Foundation plan (how it sits)

  • Options: slab, piers, frost-protected systems, etc.
  • Why it matters: frost depth, soil, drainage, and loads drive approvals.
  • Shortcut: see Foundation options (Canada).

4) Specs + inspection pathway

  • Includes: basic construction specs, insulation info, and a pathway for electrical/plumbing approvals.
  • Why it matters: “wired” is not the same as “inspection-ready.”
  • Canada note: cold-weather details (air/vapor control, winterization) are often questioned.

The “documentation test”

If a seller can’t provide model-specific drawings/specs and dodges inspection questions, treat the listing as an unknown spec. Unknown specs become unknown costs.

Call script: what to ask your municipality

Use this as your 5-minute checklist. The goal is to get a clear “what we need” list before you spend money on a unit.

Questions that unlock the whole process

  • Is this use allowed? (year-round vs seasonal, main dwelling vs accessory dwelling)
  • Any minimum size rules? (floor area, bedrooms, etc.)
  • Setbacks + height limits? (front/side/rear, lot coverage)
  • What documents are required? (site plan, drawings, foundation plan, specs)
  • What inspections apply? (electrical, plumbing, occupancy, etc.)

Questions that protect your budget

  • Foundation expectations? (slab vs piers vs engineered approach)
  • Utility servicing rules? (grid power, well/water, septic/sewer)
  • Submission format? (who can prepare plans, what scale/format)
  • Common reasons applications get rejected? (missing docs, siting issues, use mismatch)
  • Estimated timeline? (what causes delays)

What to say to the seller

“My municipality needs a site plan, drawings for this exact model, and a foundation plan. Can you provide the drawings/spec sheet for this model and any installation guidance?”

If they answer with marketing language instead of documents, that’s a signal.

Red flags (before you pay)

These don’t mean “never buy.” They mean: treat it like an unknown spec and price in risk, time, and extra work.

No drawings for the exact model

If they can’t provide basic plans/specs, you may need to create them before permits can even start.

“Fully compliant everywhere” claims

Approvals are local. Any seller promising universal approval without reviewing your site is overselling.

Inspection pathway is unclear

Electrical/plumbing “included” isn’t the same as “ready for local inspection.” Ask how it’s intended to be approved.

FAQ

Short answers, Canada-first, focused on avoiding surprises.

Do I start with the seller or the municipality?

Start with the municipality (or permitting office). Zoning, use type, minimum size, and required drawings determine whether a unit can be approved on your property.

What documents are usually requested for a building permit?

Common requests include site plan, floor plan/elevations, foundation plan, and basic specs. Some sites also require engineering or additional details depending on use type and local rules.

Is a prefab home “permit-free” because it was built in a factory?

Usually no. Even if the unit is manufactured, the placement, foundation, utility connections, and local compliance still require approvals and inspections.

What’s the #1 mistake buyers make?

Paying before confirming zoning + a foundation plan + a clear inspection pathway (especially electrical). Unknowns in these three areas are the biggest cause of delays and extra costs.
