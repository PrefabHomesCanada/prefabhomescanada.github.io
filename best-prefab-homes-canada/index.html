What Amazon listings don’t include (but your project needs)

Expandable prefab listings often show a finished interior and imply a ready-to-live experience. In practice, many buyers should plan as if they’re purchasing a structure that still requires a local build plan. Below are the most common gaps that affect Canadian buyers.

1) Foundation and frost protection

Canada’s freeze-thaw cycles and frost depth requirements make foundations a key cost driver. Many projects require an engineered base for dwellings.

2) Permits and municipal approvals

Many listings do not include stamped drawings, site plans, or a permit package. Zoning and occupancy rules vary by municipality.

3) Plumbing: water + wastewater

“Bathroom included” might mean a room or fixtures. You still need water supply and wastewater plans (well/septic or hookups).

4) Electrical service and approvals

Confirm whether components are approved for Canada (CSA/UL/ETL). Even then, trenching, panels, and hookups require local trades.

5) Insulation, air sealing, and winterization

Ask for insulation type/thickness, window specs, and sealing details. Poor air sealing can create high heating costs.

6) Snow load and roof design

Don’t rely on generic claims. Confirm roof structure and snow-load assumptions for your area.

Practical takeaway: treat the Amazon unit as one line item in a larger build plan. If you can’t get written specs, assume higher upgrade and coordination costs.

Real cost breakdown in Canada (a realistic planning range)

The “true cost” of a prefab home project is the sum of the unit, the site, and the approvals. Your numbers vary by region, access, soil, and whether the unit is treated as a full-time dwelling or accessory structure.

Delivery, offload, and placement

  • Planning range: $2,000–$15,000+ CAD
  • Higher if: cranes, rural access, winter delivery, complex placement

Foundation / engineered base

  • Planning range: $8,000–$35,000+ CAD
  • Higher if: poor soil, high water table, sloped site, full insulated slab

Permits, drawings, and inspections

  • Planning range: $1,000–$12,000+ CAD
  • Higher if: engineering stamps, multiple revisions, full dwelling approvals

Electrical

  • Planning range: $3,000–$20,000+ CAD
  • Higher if: new service, long trench, rural upgrades, off-grid integration

Plumbing: water + wastewater

  • Planning range: $5,000–$50,000+ CAD
  • Higher if: well + septic, rock excavation, remote access

Insulation upgrades + winterization

  • Planning range: $3,000–$25,000+ CAD
  • Higher if: window replacement, major retrofit, roof upgrades

Reality check: It’s common for the all-in project cost to be 2× to 4× the unit price for full-time living once site work and compliance are included.

FAQ: buying an Amazon prefab home in Canada

Are Amazon prefab homes legal to live in full-time in Canada?

Sometimes, but it depends on zoning and compliance documentation. Many listings are best treated as structures or shells unless proven otherwise.

What’s the biggest hidden cost?

Site work: foundation, permits, utilities, plumbing (especially septic/well), and winterization can exceed the unit price.

Do listings include a Canadian permit package?

Usually not. If you need stamped drawings or engineering, expect to hire local professionals.

What should I verify before I buy?

Ask for insulation specs, window/door details, roof design and snow-load assumptions, and electrical approvals (CSA/UL/ETL).

Can I use one as a backyard suite?

Possibly. Secondary suite rules can be strict (setbacks, services, inspections). Confirm with your municipality before purchasing.
